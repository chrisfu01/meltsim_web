Thermodynamic Simulator

Abstract

Simulating proper melting is difficult. Flashy models seem to lack realism, while realistic models are difficult to achieve. The edges of a proper model seem to collapse, rounding out while the faces shrink at a slower rate, due to a higher surface area at the edges. Our model renders an ice cube as a matrix of voxels. It then applies Euler’s method over the Thermodynamic Formula to calculate the next phase of an ice cube melting.

Technical Approach

Modeling

Our rendering is done in OpenGL. We used this library to create our shapes, namely the small cube voxels used to represent the pyramid, rect-prism, or cube. Using shaders, we create a much more tolerable image that has some degree of realism. We enhanced this using a shader pipeline to determine colors at each position and the opacity. We chose to use diffuse normal shading to enhance the shading at the voxel surfaces. We also have specular values for each material as well, based on their true specular value (we just used high values for metallic substances). We placed a light source at about the middle of the leftmost edge of our window.

We decided against raytracing because it simply required too much computational power. It wasn’t worth pursuing because we went a simulation route based more on realism than image quality. In addition, our baseline was ice since we had the most real-world material to compare our simulation to ice. Our research told us that rendering realistic ice was its own issue altogether.

Representation

We simulate a uniform shape that is either a cube, prism, or pyramid. We represent the object as a series of cube-shaped voxels. We assign each voxel its own state variables (temperature, liquid/solid, etc.). We then set each voxel as an edge, surface, or neither. The reasoning will be explained in the “thermodynamics” portion.

We are using a dynamic camera and key-listeners for the scene.

We are using an ambient heat environment (neutral heating from each side). The object suffers no internal heating, so the surfaces will naturally melt faster than the interior of the cube. We have to account for this in the next step.

Thermodynamics

We apply the heat transfer formula, to each voxel. Each voxel will have an area of contact with the ambient, and we can use the voxel’s state variable and the ambient temperature to progress to the next time-step. We also used standard coefficients from physics online textbooks.

We use Euler’s method to calculate work done on each voxel for a certain timestep. This will overestimate work done, but we can alleviate some of these issues with smart selection of time-step size. With the specific heat, fusion of enthalpy, and our calculations, we have enough to solve the timestep.

Results

The cube/prism melt very nicely, as does the pyramid. It simulates the proper physics and responds accordingly to the environment variables. The edges collapse in the case of a cube, so we extended this to a pyramid. We were stunned to see the results from the pyramid: it was truly fascinating!

There are some things to note, however. First, the cube produces a sort of rippling effect regardless of the color settings. We believe that this effect is due to how OpenGL assigns its normals in the shaders. However, due to the project being an emphasis on simulation and not necessarily image quality, we decided to leave it as is. There’s also an additional storage and render requirement to eliminate this effect, which was too much for our computers.

Recommendations

We would recommend this to a student who doesn’t want to produce something image-heavy and wants to produce something simulation based. We would also recommend this to students interested in physics and the real world or even physics engines (melting does happen in some games). Perhaps they can produce an even more advanced shape to melt!
